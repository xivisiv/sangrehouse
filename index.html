THE METHOD TO MADNESS

“And those who were seen dancing were thought to be insane by those who could not hear the music.” — Nietzsche

The first musicians were individuals who built a relationship with a sound they heard in nature. Be it by hitting something by accident and hearing its resonance echo, or by hearing the rhythmic beating of a motion like walking or trying to start fires with firewood.

Active listening as the first step.

Then comes relationship — a response from the mind and body. The continuance of this cycle builds the instrument that creates the relationship between listener and object that we call music. This instrument is the creative principle of music which is inherent to all beings.

As the active listener becomes trained, the object can transform from a mere physical substance to something likeemotions, ideas, and states of experience. This ongoing relationship led to the discovery of things such as the major scale, rhythm, chanting, etc.

The Three Acts of Inner Music

I

Act 1: The Active Listener

Training the Active Listener to become an instrument. The difference between a trained and untrained ear is the ability to resonate like an instrument as you hear the sound.

II

Act 2: Body Responsiveness

Developing the ability to respond, in the body, to the music outside and inside. This is done by training the body to follow the gravity of rhythm and sequential patterns (notes, scales, chords).

III

Act 3: Intentionality

The combination of listening and body response creates intentionality. We explore aesthetic choices and complex theoretical constructs to fine-tune the relationships we have with sound.

II. Inner Music / V. The Aim

Inner Music trains this original connection.

It teaches the body to hear and the ear to feel.

Every exercise begins with presence and awareness, not technique. You learn to move as sound moves, to respond as nature responds — naturally, precisely, without hesitation.

As your perception sharpens, the music starts to shape itself. Theory and structure emerge as reflections of what you already sense.

V. AIM: Tuning the Instrument

  • To tune the human instrument.
  • To restore the physical and emotional intelligence that gives rise to music.
  • To make creation an act of will.

III. Foundational Practice

  • Listening — Developing sensitivity to tone, space, rhythm, and silence.
  • Resonance — Feeling vibration in the body and learning to respond through movement.
  • Expression — Exploring how awareness becomes sound through your chosen instrument.
  • Reflection — Refining your aesthetic judgment by noticing what feels aligned, what feels false, what asks to return.

IV. What To Expect

  • Developed Ear and capacity for active listening.
  • Refined aesthetic taste (become aware of the choices you can make and how to achieve them).
  • Knowledge of music theory (as needed—assessed based on student’s needs).
  • Connection between creativity and intention so that each time you play music you can be inspired.
  • The ability to play songs of your liking.
  • Ongoing support with technical issues relevant to playing, singing, creating, and/or producing music.

The Meeting Point: Physics & Psychology

Physics of Sound

The harmonic overtone series is the foundational meeting point between psychology and physics. Musical notes are tuned to the natural resonance frequency responses apparent all around in nature.

The instrument is one piece of the puzzle. The way it’s played, the emotional state of the player, the room it resonates in, and how the musician is listening to the sound echoing back all create an intricate relationship that gives rise to the creative principle.

This won’t be a course on the math, but an overview of the fundamental principles that are relevant for a musician to know.

The Art of Friction

Every artist must become well-versed in the art of FRICTION.

No authentic and self-expressive work of art was written without some sort of friction building the gravity for it.

With Inner Music, we build sufficient understanding of how to direct the frustration of learning and creating toward the goal of self-expression and artistry.
